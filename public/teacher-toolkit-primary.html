Teacher Toolkit — Primary (Ages 8–11)

Practical lesson plans, student activities, and simple templates to introduce AI concepts safely and accessibly.

Overview (1 page)

This toolkit gives teachers three short lessons suitable for ages 8–11. Each lesson is activity-led and emphasises critical thinking, ethics, and simple technical intuition—not coding.

Lesson 1 — What is AI? (30–45 minutes)

Learning goals

  • Understand that "AI" means systems that make decisions from data or rules.
  • Give examples of everyday AI (recommendations, voice assistants, simple filters).

Materials

  • Printed card pack: pictures & descriptions (provided below)
  • Whiteboard / flipchart

Card pack (printable)

Cut these into cards for the matching activity. Each card has a short description and a simple prompt for students.

Image: Recommender

Video recommender

Suggests videos based on what you watched before. Prompt: How does it decide which videos to show?
Image: Voice Assistant

Voice assistant

Answers questions and follows voice commands. Prompt: When might it make mistakes?
Image: Face Recogniser

Face recogniser

Finds faces in photos. Prompt: What could go wrong if it confuses people?

Activity

  1. Show students images or examples and ask: "Is this an example of AI?" Discuss.
  2. Group work: match pictures to simple descriptions (e.g., "recommends videos", "recognises faces").
  3. Wrap-up: Write one sentence about where students have seen these tools in their daily lives.

Teacher notes

Keep definitions short and concrete. Focus on cause/effect rather than technical details. Emphasise that AI is built by people and can be improved.

Lesson 2 — Data & Bias (30–45 minutes)

Learning goals

  • Understand that AI systems learn from examples (data).
  • Recognise that if data is unbalanced, outputs can be unfair.

Activity

  1. Show two short stories about an automated decision; ask students which feels fair and why.
  2. Class experiment: use token sets (coloured balls) to demonstrate sampling and how results change. See detailed teacher instructions below.
  3. Discuss: how would we check an AI system to make sure it is fair?

Teacher notes

Avoid complex statistics—use concrete visual metaphors. Reassure students that being critical of technology is a positive skill.

Class experiment — step-by-step (teacher script)

Goal

Show how different samples of data can change the behaviour of a system and why it matters for fairness.

Materials
  • Two or three sets of coloured tokens (e.g., ping-pong balls, counters, or printed tokens). Choose contrasting colours like red/blue/green. ~30 tokens per set works well.
  • Two opaque bags or boxes to draw tokens from (or use a covered hat).
  • Whiteboard and marker to record results.
  • Optional: sticky notes to label groups or counters on the board.
Setup
  1. Prepare two bags with different mixes of tokens. Example: Bag A = 20 red, 10 blue; Bag B = 10 red, 20 blue. (Red = outcome X, Blue = outcome Y.)
  2. Explain that an 'automatic system' will make a decision by randomly sampling a token from a bag and using the token colour as the output.
  3. Split the class into small teams (3–5 students) to collect samples and record observations.
Script (what to say)

"Today we'll pretend a computer makes decisions by looking at past examples. Imagine the tokens show what the system usually saw before. We'll draw tokens to see how the system behaves."

"Team 1 will draw 10 tokens from Bag A and count how many reds and blues they get. Team 2 will do the same with Bag B. We'll compare results."

Run the experiment
  1. Each team draws 10 tokens at random from their assigned bag and records counts (red vs blue).
  2. Collect the team results on the board and calculate totals and proportions (e.g., 7/10 red = 70% red).
  3. Repeat the draw a couple of times (different students) to show variation between samples.
Expected observations to highlight
  • Bag A tends to produce more red; Bag B tends to produce more blue — this shows how the underlying data influences outputs.
  • Different draws (samples) vary — even from the same bag you may get different counts; randomness matters.
  • A small sample may be misleading; larger samples reduce random variation.
Discussion prompts (for non-technical teachers)
  • What does each bag represent in real life? (e.g., past decisions, user data, historical records)
  • If a computer learned only from Bag A history, what decisions might it make more often? Would that be fair for everyone?
  • How could we check if a real system is biased? (Look at examples it makes mistakes on, compare results for different groups.)
  • What would you change in the system to make it fairer? (Collect better data, add human checks, monitor performance regularly.)
Teacher tips
  • Keep language simple: use 'examples' instead of 'training data' when addressing younger students.
  • Use concrete analogies: 'the bag shows what the computer has seen before.'
  • If time is short, do one round and discuss the idea rather than repeating draws.

Lesson 3 — Responsible Use & Everyday Decisions (30–45 minutes)

Learning goals

  • Discuss choices about when to use automation and when humans should decide.
  • Introduce simple checks: accuracy, explainability, human oversight.

Activity

  1. Scenario role-play: students act as decision panels for an automated tool (e.g., library book recommender).
  2. Debrief: what rules would you set? When should a human step in?

Teacher notes

Emphasise values like fairness, transparency and human judgement. Offer extension tasks for older classes (research local AI systems).

Printable student worksheet (1 page)

Worksheet: Describe an example of AI you've used. Circle whether it felt fair, helpful, or surprising. Suggest one way to make it better.
